What Is EB Garamond?

In 2011 the Austrian designer Georg Mayr-Duffner[2] released the EB Garamond under the Open Font License. Mayr-Duffner took the letterforms from a scan of a specimen known as the “Berner specimen” which was printed in 1592 by Conrad Berner, son-in-law of Christian Egenolff and his successor at the Egenolff print office. It shows Garamont's roman and Granjon's italic fonts at different sizes. The Greek characters are based on Robert Granjon's work as well. In addition the font includes OpenType features such as swash italic capitals and schoolbook alternates.[3] Duffner's intention was to include multiple optical sizes. As of 2014 his implementation included fonts based on the 8 and 12 point forms from the 1592 specimen, but lacked the bold font faces. As Georg Mayr-Duffner couldn't complete the bold weights for personal reasons, Google commissioned the Spanish type designer Octavio Pardo[4] to continue the project. As of 2018 Pardo's implementation includes 5 weights (Regular, Medium, Semi-Bold, Bold and Extra-Bold), both in regular and italic style.

How And Why Was EB Garamond Created?

Georg Duffner, the principle designer of EB Garamond began EB Garamond with the intent for it to be an excellent, classical, Garamond. EB Garamond has been a community project to create a reimagination of Claude Garamont's humanist typefaces from the sixteenth century. This digital version very closely replicates the original design made by Claude Garamont. The letterform source was scanned from the "Berner specimien," created in 1592 by Conrad Berner who was the son in law of Christian Egenolff at the Egenolff print office. The name of this project, EB Garamond comes from the specific involvment of Garamont's roman and Granjon's italic types at different sizes.

Why another Garamond?
Garamond was a vital moment within the history of typography. European type designers have been augmenting and complimenting this work evern since its creation. There have been numerous revisions of Garamound and many of which are considered to higher end typfaces, however in the modern day culter of free readily accessible digital conent, very few Garamond style typefaces exist, and none have reached the scope or popularity of this project.

What Is Garamond?

Garamond is a large group of serif typefaces that got their name from sixteenth-century Parisian engraver Claude Garamond. Garamond’s types followed the model of a typeface cut for venetian printer Aldus Manutius. These are now known as old-style of serif letter design. Garamond garnered a large popularity surge in the eighteenth and nineteenth century resulting in many revival faces with a modern twist being developed. Garamond has been praised as an elegant and stylized typeface, exemplifying a renaissance design philosophy. Many modern interpretations of Garamond seek to modernize these aspects, often adding matching bold and lining to capital numbers and letters, aspects that were not present in the renaissance.
